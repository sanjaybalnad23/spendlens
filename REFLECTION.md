# REFLECTION

## 1. The hardest bug/problem I hit this week

Hardest thing by far was the audit engine itself.

At first I thought:
“just write some rules and calculate savings”

Yeah no

Very quickly I realized AI SaaS pricing and usage-fit logic is insanely difficult and subjective. I spent a lot of time researching rule-based engine architecture and how production systems handle recommendation logic. I explored some rule engine libraries too (like json-rules-engine and similar ones), but most of them felt too abstract or overkill for this project.

The hardest part was not coding the engine technically. It was deciding whether the recommendations actually made sense from a business perspective.

For example:

- when is a business plan actually overkill?
- when does absolute team size matter?
- when should adoption ratio matter more?
- when is “remove tool” too aggressive?
- when should the engine honestly say “this stack is already optimized”?

I debugged mostly by manually passing audit inputs into the engine repeatedly and inspecting outputs. A lot of the rules got rewritten multiple times after seeing weird recommendations.

Even now, I honestly think the engine is still inaccurate in many situations. Real procurement optimization is extremely hard. But I think the final system is at least transparent and explainable instead of pretending to be magical AI.

---

## 2. A decision I reversed mid-week

Initially I wanted to make the audit engine AI-driven.

After thinking more about it and rereading the assignment, I realized using AI for actual savings logic would probably make the recommendations inconsistent and hallucinated.

So I completely reversed direction and built a deterministic rule-based engine instead.

AI is only used for generating the personalized summary paragraph now.

I think this was the correct decision because the audit logic became much more explainable and debuggable.

I also almost started using a heavy rule engine library but later decided to keep the engine simple and custom-built because I understood the logic better that way.

## 4. How I used AI tools

I used ChatGPT heavily during development.

Mostly for:

- brainstorming architecture
- discussing rule-engine design
- debugging TypeScript issues
- reviewing recommendation logic
- improving UI polish
- generating/refining prompts
- understanding tradeoffs

I did NOT trust AI with:

- pricing accuracy
- business reasoning
- architecture decisions blindly
- recommendation correctness

One specific time AI was wrong was around the recommendation rules. It initially generated overly aggressive downgrade logic that looked technically correct but made no real business sense.

For example, it assumed small teams should almost never use business plans, which is obviously not always true. That led me to rethink the engine architecture and combine adoption ratio with absolute organizational scale instead of using naive rules.

I also caught multiple TypeScript and Mongoose typing mistakes generated by AI.

Overall AI was extremely useful as a thinking partner, but I still had to validate almost everything manually.
